Here's the vision for Line Shark's UI:
The vibe: Bloomberg terminal meets a high-end casino floor. Dark, moody, data-dense — but everything moves. It should feel like you're looking at a living, breathing organism of sports data. Think the interfaces in Westworld or Minority Report but grounded and usable.
Color system: Near-black background (#06080f), not pure black. Cards float on a slightly lighter surface (#0f1320). Two signal colors dominate everything — electric green (#00ff87) for BUY and hot pink-red (#ff3366) for BYE. Cyan (#00d4ff) as an accent for neutral data. Everything else is shades of slate gray. No other colors. The restraint is what makes it feel premium.
The background: A subtle animated mesh gradient that slowly breathes behind everything. Think two or three large, blurred color blobs (dark green and dark blue) that drift around on a 30-second loop. Barely noticeable consciously, but it makes the whole app feel alive. Layer a film grain/noise texture on top at like 3% opacity so it doesn't look sterile.
Typography: Two fonts only. A tight, heavy grotesque for headlines — something like Satoshi or General Sans at 900 weight with negative letter-spacing so the text feels compressed and urgent. A monospace font for all data and numbers — JetBrains Mono or Fira Code. Numbers should always feel like they're coming off a data feed. Body text in the same grotesque at 400 weight.
The loading/analysis sequence is the hero moment. When the user hits "Analyze," the entire screen transforms. The sport cards dissolve away and a vertical pipeline visualization takes over. Three nodes connected by a line, each representing a stage (Scrape → Intel → Analyze). As each stage runs, the connecting line animates with a flowing particle effect — little dots of light traveling along the path like data moving through a wire. Each node pulses with a ring animation when active, then fills solid green when complete. The current stage has a glitch/flicker effect on its label text, like it's processing. Below the pipeline, a live terminal-style log scrolls with monospace text showing what's actually happening: "Scraping covers.com/nba/odds..." and "Found 8 games..." — this makes it feel real and technical, not like a fake loading spinner.
Game cards enter with staggered choreography. When results load, cards don't just appear. The screen transitions with a fast horizontal wipe, then cards cascade in from the bottom with a spring physics animation — each one 80ms after the last, with a slight overshoot and settle. BUY cards have a faint green glow on their left border that pulses once on entry. BYE cards are dimmer, more muted — they visually recede so your eye goes to the action.
Each game card is a mini-dashboard. The matchup names are large and bold at the top. Below that, a row of stat chips — small rounded rectangles showing the line, odds, and bet type in monospace. The confidence score isn't just a number — it's a horizontal bar that fills with a gradient animation on entry, green-to-cyan for BUY, red-to-orange for BYE. The BUY/BYE badge in the top right corner has a subtle background pulse animation, like a heartbeat. Bet size and potential payout are displayed large, in the signal color, with a counting-up number animation when they first appear (like an odometer rolling).
Expand interaction: Tap a card and it doesn't just toggle open. The card smoothly expands with a spring animation, pushing cards below it down. The detail content fades in with a 150ms delay so the expansion finishes before the text appears. Inside, key factors have green arrow markers and risk factors have red ones. The historical edge section has a small inline sparkline chart if there's trend data — a tiny line graph showing the team's recent performance as a visual pulse.
Hover states everywhere. Cards lift slightly (translateY -2px) with a box shadow that intensifies. Buttons have a glow effect that spreads from the center outward. The sport selection tiles scale up 2% on hover with their border brightening. Interactive elements have a custom cursor — a small crosshair or dot instead of the default pointer, reinforcing the precision/tactical feel.
The summary bar at the top of results has three stat boxes: total BUY signals, total BYE signals, and total recommended action (dollar amount). These numbers animate in with a slot-machine roll effect — digits cycling rapidly before landing on the final number. The total action number has the green-to-cyan gradient text treatment.
Micro-interactions that add life: When you toggle between sports, the icon does a quick 360-degree rotation. The "Re-Analyze" button has a rotating refresh icon that spins while loading. Scrolling the results page causes a parallax effect on the background mesh gradient — it moves at 20% of scroll speed, adding depth. The header logo "LINE SHARK" has a subtle shimmer animation — a diagonal light sweep that passes across the text every 8 seconds.
Mobile considerations: Cards go full-width and stack vertically. The sport selector becomes a horizontal scroll strip instead of a grid. The analysis pipeline goes vertical with the terminal log below it. Swipe left on a game card to dismiss/hide BYE games you don't care about. Pull-to-refresh triggers a re-analysis.
One more detail: When a BUY signal has confidence 8 or above, the entire card gets a special treatment — a thin animated border that cycles through the green-to-cyan gradient, like a glowing outline tracing the perimeter of the card on a 3-second loop. This is your "hot pick" indicator. It immediately draws the eye and creates a visual hierarchy without needing a separate label.
The whole thing should feel like a weapon. Fast, dark, precise. Every animation serves a purpose — either guiding attention, conveying state, or making data entry feel satisfying. Nothing decorative for its own sake.
